On the face of it, this property presents itself as a fantastic option. Its most immediate advantage is its sheer size, offering the potential for between 10 and 13 bedrooms, and it appears to be in great overall condition, requiring some work but nothing too substantial. The interiors are quite impressive, particularly the bathrooms, which have already been wonderfully redone. Structurally, the house is also appealing; unlike the vast majority of stone houses in the region, this one is a more modern construction of concrete and steel, with the ground floor built in the 1950s and the first floor expanded in the 1970s. It features a practical layout with one bathroom per floor, good stairs, and a garage that could easily be converted into a laboratory or workshop. The setting is another major draw, with the Bormida river running right next to the property. This offers the exciting possibility of creating a natural-like pond for hot days or using the water to supply a swimming pool. The large, flat, and already-fenced front garden is a versatile space, and the covered entrance area—the *porticato*—is a lovely feature that could be enclosed to create an adaptable indoor-outdoor room.

This potential directly inspires some dream builds. The most compelling idea would be to create a major external kitchen within the enclosed *porticato*. This would provide a huge, independent cooking space for everyone while maximising the number of private bedrooms within the main house. Enclosing it would also ensure privacy and warmth during the colder months. Furthermore, a small 4x2 metre cellar (*cantina*) on the ground floor, level with the river, is perfectly positioned to become a beautiful sauna with a river view.

However, there are some significant problems to consider. The property is located right next to a provincial road (*strada provinciale*), which, while likely quiet at night, will be highly trafficated during the day, creating considerable noise. The house itself is well-insulated from this—with double-glazed windows and a second set of windows behind them—so the interior remains quiet. The garden, however, would suffer from a noisy environment. One could move the main relaxation area down towards the riverbed, but this introduces its own risks. The river is the most serious potential threat; flooding is a common problem in Liguria, and if the house were to be flooded, it would be catastrophic. The fact that the villa is built directly on the riverbed raises questions about its long-term structural integrity, a concern underscored by a suspicious crack that is already visible. One would not want that to develop into something more substantial over the years.
